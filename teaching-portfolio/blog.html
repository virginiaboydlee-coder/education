Music Education PYP Reflection

Finding the Inquiry in Every Song

March 12, 2025 · 5 min read

Last Tuesday, a Grade 4 student asked me: "Why does minor sound sad?" I didn't answer. Instead, I turned the question back to the class — and what followed was forty minutes of the most genuine musical inquiry I've witnessed in years. Here's what I learned from not having the answer.

The IB PYP invites us to teach through provocation, not instruction. But in music, I sometimes default to explanation — especially when I know the "right" answer. That Tuesday reminded me that the richest learning happens when I hold back and trust the students' natural inclination to wonder. The minor key question sparked a student-led listening study that lasted three sessions and culminated in a class-composed piece titled "The Blue Room." I didn't plan any of it. I just stayed curious alongside them.

Exhibition Reflection PYP

Reflecting on Exhibition Season: What Students Teach Me

April 28, 2025 · 6 min read

Exhibition season is equal parts exhausting and exhilarating. As a mentor, I walk alongside students through their most significant independent inquiry of primary school — and every year, I am changed by what I witness.

This year, one student's inquiry into mental health stigma led her to write a song that she initially refused to share. "It's too personal," she said. We talked about the relationship between vulnerability and connection in art. Three weeks later, she performed it at Exhibition night to a standing ovation. Witnessing a child trust their own voice — in the most literal sense — is why I became a music teacher. The Exhibition doesn't just showcase student learning. It reveals who they are becoming.

Assessment Reflection

Assessment as Learning: Rethinking How We Measure Musical Growth

February 3, 2025 · 7 min read

I used to hand students a rubric after they performed. This year, I started handing it to them before — and asking them to co-write the success criteria. The shift has been profound.

When students own the assessment criteria, they stop performing for the teacher and start performing for themselves. A Grade 5 student put it perfectly during a self-assessment conference: "I gave myself an 'achieving' because I can play it cleanly, but I don't feel it yet — and feeling it is on the rubric because I put it there." That sentence contains more musical intelligence than any test I could design. Co-constructed assessment is not just more equitable — it is more accurate.

IB Cat 3 Workshop Takeaways: Leading for Learning

November 18, 2024 · 4 min read

Three days in Bangkok surrounded by educators from 14 countries. Here are the ideas that are still rattling around in my head two weeks later.

The most challenging moment came during a session on distributed leadership. A facilitator asked: "What would your programme look like if you weren't in it?" My honest answer was uncomfortable. It pushed me to think about how I'm building capacity in colleagues rather than simply executing my own vision. The workshop reframed leadership not as authority but as the creation of conditions in which others can lead. I came home with a clearer sense of my role — and a longer to-do list.

Student Agency PYP Reflection

Student Agency in the Music Room: What It Actually Looks Like

January 14, 2025 · 5 min read

"Agency" is one of the most-used words in IB professional development. But what does it look like at 9:15 on a Tuesday morning when you have 24 Grade 3 students and 45 minutes?

I've been experimenting with "music menus" — structured choice boards where students select their own learning pathway through a unit. For our World Music unit, students could choose between listening analysis, instrument performance, creative composition, or research and presentation. What surprised me: the students who previously disengaged gravitated toward creative composition and produced the most ambitious work. Agency didn't create chaos. It created investment. The lesson I keep relearning: when students choose, they care.

World Music Music Education Reflection

Teaching World Music Without Tokenism

October 7, 2024 · 8 min read

It's easy to "do" world music in a way that reduces rich traditions to a five-minute activity. I've been guilty of it myself. Here's how I've been trying to do better.

The turning point came when a student from Ghana gently corrected my pronunciation of a Twi word during a lesson. She did it quietly and kindly — but it stopped me. I realised I had been presenting her culture's music without her voice at the centre. We redesigned the unit together. She became a co-teacher for two sessions. Her grandmother sent a voice note from Accra explaining the context of a drum pattern. That voice note became the provocation for the entire unit the following year. World music education done well amplifies the voices that are already in the room.

Music Education Assessment

Portfolios Over Tests: Documenting Growth in the Music Classroom

September 5, 2024 · 6 min read

I have never given a written music test. Not because tests are wrong, but because they can't capture what matters most in music: the process of becoming.

This year every student in Grade 4 and 5 maintains a digital music portfolio using SeeSaw. Each week they add one artefact: a recording, a photo of their notation, a written reflection, a video of rehearsal. By the end of the year, the portfolios tell a story no rubric can. One student played back her very first ukulele attempt from September next to her December performance and cried — not from embarrassment, but from pride. That moment of self-recognition is the most authentic assessment I know.

Student Agency Exhibition

When a Song Becomes Action: Music & the Exhibition

May 2, 2025 · 5 min read

The PYP exhibition requires students to take action. For most subjects, "action" means posters, petitions, or community events. In music, action can mean something more elemental — a song.

This year, a group inquiring into ocean plastic pollution composed a three-minute song called "Drift." They recorded it in our school studio, created a simple music video using ocean footage, and uploaded it to the school community page. Within a week it had been viewed 400 times and shared to three parent networks. The song was their action — and it reached further than any poster on a hallway wall. This is why I believe songwriting belongs at the heart of the Exhibition cycle, not at the edges.
